Social Security General

The fastest way is online at ssa.gov/applyforbenefits (over 70% of applications are now submitted this way). You can also call 1-800-772-1213 or visit a local office. Start your application up to 4 months before you want payments to begin. Tip: Create a my Social Security account first to pre-fill your information and see your earnings record.

The official 2026 Cost-of-Living Adjustment is 2.8%. It took effect in January 2026, raising the average retirement benefit by about $56 to $2,071/month. This is the fifth-highest COLA in the past decade. Full history and details: ssa.gov/cola.

Instantly online through your my Social Security account. It’s accepted for loans, housing, jobs, and more. Over 10 million letters are issued yearly. You can also request by phone or mail.

Yes — within the first 12 months of receiving retirement benefits, you can withdraw the application and repay everything received (including Medicare premiums). After 12 months, withdrawal is no longer allowed, but you can voluntarily suspend benefits after reaching Full Retirement Age (FRA).

The Old-Age and Survivors Insurance (OASI) Trust Fund is projected to be able to pay full scheduled benefits until 2035, after which it could pay about 77–80% without legislative changes. Congress has always acted in the past to ensure full payments — Social Security has never missed a payment in its 90+ year history.

Your benefit is based on your 35 highest-earning years (after indexing for inflation). The SSA calculates your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA) using a progressive formula with bend points. The higher your lifetime earnings, the higher your benefit — up to the 2026 maximum of $4,152 at FRA.

You earn up to 4 credits per year (1 credit per $1,890 in 2026 covered earnings). Most people need 40 credits (10 years of work) for retirement benefits. Disability and survivor benefits may require fewer recent credits.

Retirement Benefits

FRA is 66–67 depending on birth year: 66 for those born 1943–1954, rising gradually to 67 for those born 1960 or later. Use the official calculator: ssa.gov. Fact: Delaying past FRA until 70 earns 8% per year delayed retirement credits — up to 32% higher monthly benefit.

It depends on your health, life expectancy, and savings. Claiming at 62 permanently reduces your benefit by ~30%. Waiting until 70 can increase it by 24–32%. The average break-even age is around 78–80. If you expect to live past 82, delaying usually pays off significantly more.

Yes! Before FRA, the 2026 earnings limit is $24,480 ($1 withheld for every $2 earned above). In the year you reach FRA, the limit is $65,160 ($1 withheld for every $3 above until FRA month). No limit after FRA. Withheld amounts are later credited back as a higher monthly benefit.

$4,152/month at Full Retirement Age for someone who earned the maximum taxable wages for 35 years. At age 70 it reaches up to $5,181/month. Average benefit after the 2.8% COLA is $2,071/month. Check your personalized estimate at my Social Security.

Before FRA: $24,480/year. Year reaching FRA: $65,160. After FRA: no limit. These limits are adjusted annually.

Medicare

Initial Enrollment Period is 7 months around your 65th birthday (3 months before, the month of, and 3 months after). Missing it triggers a permanent 10% per year late-enrollment penalty on Part B. Tip: If you have employer coverage from a company with 20+ employees, you can delay Part B without penalty.

Standard monthly premium is $202.90 (up from $185 in 2025). High earners pay IRMAA surcharges ($74–$689+ extra based on 2024 income). Annual deductible is $283. Full IRMAA tables: Medicare.gov.

Under the Inflation Reduction Act, out-of-pocket prescription drug costs are capped at $2,000 per year (continues in 2026). No more catastrophic phase — major savings for people on expensive medications.

Medicare Advantage (Part C) often has $0 premium and extras like dental/vision/hearing but uses networks and prior authorizations. Original Medicare + Medigap offers broader provider choice and more predictable costs but higher premiums. Over 50% of beneficiaries chose Advantage in 2026.

Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount (IRMAA) is an extra surcharge on Part B (and Part D) for higher-income beneficiaries based on your tax return from two years prior. 2026 surcharges range from $81 to $689+ per month.

Medicaid

Eligibility varies by state. In expansion states, adults can qualify up to ~138% of the Federal Poverty Level (~$21,500 for a single person). Children and pregnant women often qualify at higher levels. SSI recipients are usually automatically eligible. Check your state at Medicaid.gov.

Yes — known as “dual eligible.” Medicaid often pays Medicare premiums, deductibles, and copays. The Qualified Medicare Beneficiary (QMB) program is the most common dual-eligible pathway.

Disability (SSDI)

You must have a severe disability expected to last at least 12 months or result in death, plus sufficient work credits (usually 40 total, with 20 in the last 10 years). Average initial decision wait time is 3–6 months. ~60% of initial claims are denied — appeals often succeed.

$1,630/month after the 2.8% COLA. Maximum is the same as retirement ($4,152 at FRA). Payments are based on your lifetime earnings. Check your estimate in your my Social Security account.

Yes. You get a 9-month Trial Work Period (any earnings over $1,210/month count). Then a 36-month Extended Period of Eligibility where benefits continue unless earnings exceed Substantial Gainful Activity ($1,690/month non-blind in 2026). Report all work to SSA immediately.

SSDI has a 5-month waiting period after disability onset before benefits begin. There is no waiting period for ALS or if you are already on SSDI and have a new disability.

Supplemental Security Income (SSI)

Federal maximum is $994/month for an individual and $1,491/month for a couple (after 2.8% COLA). Many states add supplements. Payments are reduced by countable income and living arrangements.

SSI is a needs-based program for low-income individuals (no work history required) and usually includes automatic Medicaid. SSDI is a work-based insurance program that requires credits and provides Medicare after 24 months of benefits.

Yes. The first $85 of earnings plus half of the remainder are excluded. Example: $400 in wages → $157.50 countable → SSI reduced by $157.50. Always report work immediately.

$2,000 for an individual and $3,000 for a couple. Certain items (home, one car, household goods) are excluded. Exceeding these limits can make you ineligible.

Survivor Benefits

Widows/widowers (age 60+, or 50+ if disabled), children under 18 (or 19 if in school), disabled adult children, and dependent parents age 62+. The deceased worker must have earned enough credits.

A widow(er) at FRA receives 100% of the deceased’s benefit. At age 60 it’s 71.5–99%. Children receive 75%. A family maximum applies. Example: Deceased received $2,000 → widow at FRA gets $2,000; child gets $1,500.

Yes. You can switch to the higher of your own benefit or the survivor benefit when it’s advantageous (usually at FRA).

Taxes on Benefits

Up to 85% of your benefits may be taxable depending on combined income (AGI + nontaxable interest + ½ of benefits). Thresholds: $25,000 single / $32,000 joint for 50% taxation; $34,000 / $44,000 for 85%. A new $6,000 senior deduction (age 65+) helps many filers.

Request voluntary withholding (7–22%) with Form W-4V and submit it to SSA. Or make quarterly estimated tax payments. Most states do not tax Social Security benefits — check your state’s rules.

Up to 3 times per year and 10 times lifetime (exceptions for name changes, work authorization updates, or compelling reasons do not count toward the limit). It’s always free.

Shelter & Housing Assistance

Call 2-1-1 (24/7) or use HUD’s Find Shelter tool: hud.gov/find_shelter. Red Cross shelters are available during disasters — no ID required.

A HUD voucher program that helps low-income families pay rent (you pay roughly 30% of income). Waitlists are long in many areas — apply through your local Public Housing Agency (PHA).

Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) apartments, Project-Based Rental Assistance, and state-specific programs. Many accept SSI/SSDI recipients. Contact 211 or your local PHA.
